"Decision Making, Emotion Recognition and Childhood Traumatic Experiences in Murder Convicts Imprisoned with Aggravated Life Sentence: A Prison Study"



A Case Study of Impairments in Decision-Making & Emotion Recognition

Decision-making and emotion recognition are both very important things humans need for cognitive function. When these two abilities are impaired, they can lead to problems in relationships, social functioning, and even legal issues, which affect both individuals and society.

Impairments in decision making and emotion recognition can result in significant social problems like ostracization, a likelihood to engage in criminal behavior, and the development of psychiatric disorders. Disorders like antisocial personality disorder (ASPD) and psychopathy, in particular, have a high association with lack of empathy, impulsivity, and alexithymia (difficulty in recognizing and expressing one’s own emotions). All of these symptoms contribute to increased aggression, brutality, and violent conduct, which in turn often leads to criminal activity. As a result, these disorders can pose a serious threat not only to the individuals themselves but also to society, including legal trouble, social rejection, and mental health issues.

Antisocial personality disorder has been strongly linked to criminal behavior and creates major challenges for mental health professionals and society when it comes to understanding, predicting, and treating the disorder.

Psychopathy, which is closely related to antisocial personality disorder (ASPD) is also strongly linked to criminal behavior, aggression, and developmental issues. It’s influenced by both environmental and genetic factors, and most people with psychopathy (about 80–90%) also meet the criteria for ASPD. However, only a smaller group of people with ASPD (around 25–40%) are considered to have psychopathy.

It’s important to understand the underlying factors that contribute to the behavior of individuals with psychopathy and ASPD, especially in the context of serious criminal offenses.
