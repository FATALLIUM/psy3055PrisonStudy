"Decision Making, Emotion Recognition and Childhood Traumatic Experiences in Murder Convicts Imprisoned with Aggravated Life Sentence: A Prison Study"


Participants, instruments, and data acquisition

Study Design

Participants:

  • Inmate Group: 23 incarcerated males serving life sentences for brutal murder (Turkish Penal Code).
  • Control Group: 23 non-criminal males matched for age, sex, and education.
  • Setting: Prison No. 7, with three psychiatric interviews per inmate.
  • Ethics: Approved by Istanbul University’s Ethics Committee; written consent obtained.

Inclusion and exclusion criteria

Inclusion (Inmate group):

  • Diagnosis of antisocial personality disorder (ASPD) (DSM-V).
  • Psychopathy score ≥ 25 (Hare Psychopathy Checklist-Revised, PCL-R).
  • Age 18-60, literate, voluntary participation.

Exclusion (Both groups):

  • Schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, generalized anxiety disorder.
  • Major depressive disorder (active treatment)..
  • Substance addiction..
  • Chronic physical/neurological conditions affecting mental state.

Tests and scales

Test Purpose
Defense Style Questionnaire (DSQ) Iowa Gambling Test (IGT) Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R) Toronto Alexithymia Scale (TAS-20) Childhood Trauma Questionnaire (CTQ) Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test (RMET)
Assesses unconscious psychological defense mechanisms to understand how individuals cope with stress and conflict. Categorizes defenses into primitive (e.g., denial, projection), neurotic (e.g., idealization), and mature (e.g., humor, sublimation) (Andrews). Designed by Bechara and Damasio in 1944. Evaluates real-world decision-making and impulse control by simulating risk-reward scenarios. Participants choose between high-risk/high-reward (decks A/B) and low-risk/stable-reward (decks C/D) options. Gold-standard diagnostic tool for psychopathy, measuring Factor 1 (emotional detachment, grandiosity) and Factor 2 (antisocial behavior, impulsivity). A score of 0-8 is deemed very low psychopathy. 9-16 is low; 17-24, medium; 25-32, high; 33-40, very high (levels 1-5). Cutoff score in Europe: ≥ 25 = high psychopathy (Tutuncu et al). Alexithymia is when a person has difficulty recognizing, expressing, and describing one’s emotions. It is not a condition that is classified in DSM-V. Measures emotional processing deficits, specifically difficulty identifying, describing, and distinguishing emotions. TAS-20 is scored on a 1-5 point (strongly agree to strongly disagree) Likert scale. A Likert scale is a rating consisting of a question or statement followed by a series of answer questions ranging from one extreme attitude to another (e.g. “Strongly disagree”, “Disagree”, “Neutral”, etc). The total score for this test ranges from 20-100 by summing the scores of the 20 items in which higher scores indicate more severe alexithymia symptoms (Seo et al). Quantifies early-life abuse and neglect across five domains: emotional, physical, and sexual abuse, plus emotional and physical neglect. The test also includes 3 questions as a minimization factor to see if participants are underplaying or denying their traumatic experiences. RMET is designed to measure the theory of mind. The theory of mind is the ability to recognize and understand another person’s mental state. The test consists of 36 photos, each showing a pair of eyes along with 4 choices of words to describe what the person in the picture might be thinking or feeling.
